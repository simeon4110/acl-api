Scope

This database aims to offer every sonnet in the English language, from the sixteenth century to the present, in interoperable formats. We began in May 2018 with the Norton anthology The Making of a Sonnet; expanded in June 2018 to Project Gutenberg books; and have since begun to incorporate every sonnet in the EEBO-TCP corpus of English books printed from 1475-1700 (Phases 1 and 2).

Questions

Sonnets are reducible neither to formal definitions (14 lines of rhymed iambic pentameter) nor to generic definitions (first-person reflection or argument, often with a volta): the many exceptions to these ‘rules’ make sonnets both flexible and prodigious. But how would our understanding of sonnets change were we to move beyond major authors (Wyatt, Shakespeare, Milton) and anthologized selections to read every sonnet printed in English?

For instance, is the sonnet a form, or a genre? What rhyme scheme do most sonnets use? Who is the most innovative sonneteer, and who the most typical? And what does the ground truth of all those sonnets do to our standard definitions of this form-genre hybrid?

Methods

We are quantifying and comparing the linguistic features of sonnets using the Natural Language Toolkit. We have made a number of tools available via our Application Program Interface (API); you can read the documentation here, including our gentle introduction to Python programming — for humanists who may never have written a line of code.
